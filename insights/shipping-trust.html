Shipping is a trust exercise

1 April 2026

Users do not owe us their patience. Every launch asks them to trust that we will not waste their time, leak their data or leave them stuck in support limbo. That trust is built in boring details long before marketing writes a headline.

What I verify before we flip the switch

  • Instrumentation: event names, funnels and dashboards match the decision we intend to make in the first week.
  • Support: macros, escalation paths and “known issues” copy exist so frontline teams are not improvising.
  • Rollback: feature flags or safe revert paths are rehearsed, not theoretical.
  • The “why” sentence: one plain-language line customers see that explains the benefit, not internal project codenames.

Why this matters on marketplaces

Money and reputation move together. A clever fee layout that saves engineering time can silently erode trust. I would rather ship a smaller surface with honest copy than a wide surface that needs footnotes.

What I learned

Checklists are not bureaucracy when they shorten the distance between intent and reality. The teams I like working with treat launch hygiene as respect for users — and for each other.
